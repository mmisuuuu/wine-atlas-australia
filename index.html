From Vine to Glass

An interactive atlas of Australia's wine regions: where the grapes grow, what experts think of the result, and how climate quietly shapes everything in between.

64 wine regions · 2,329 expert reviews · Six states · One continent

By Denise Thania·Student ID 34442359·FIT2179 · 2026

Australia produces more than a billion litres of wine each year, almost two-thirds of it exported. Behind that figure sits a remarkably uneven landscape: a single inland region in South Australia crushes more grapes than Tasmania, Queensland and Western Australia put together — yet some of the country's most celebrated bottles come from cool, marginal climates that barely register on production tables. This atlas brings together two authoritative datasets — Wine Australia's official Geographical Indications and 2,329 WineEnthusiast expert reviews — to map that contrast across seven interactive views. Each chart answers one question; together they tell the story of a wine industry built on a paradox between volume and quality.

About This Project

Domain
Australian wine production, regional character, and expert assessment, 2020–2025.
Audience
Australian wine consumers, hospitality professionals, and viticulture students seeking to understand the structure of the domestic wine industry.
Why
Australian wine is widely consumed but rarely understood as a geographic and climatic system. This atlas makes the structure of the industry visible.
How
Two open datasets cleaned in Python, joined on region name, and visualised in Vega-Lite. Map shapes from Wine Australia's official Open Data Hub.

Where Australia Makes Wine

Australia recognises 64 official wine regions, known as Geographical Indications. They cluster along a thin southern crescent that runs from Margaret River in the west, through the Adelaide Hills and Barossa, across to the Yarra Valley, the Hunter, and down into Tasmania. Almost nothing grows in the interior — the central two-thirds of the continent are too hot, too arid, and too far from water to support viticulture.

The map below colours each region by its 2024 grape crush in tonnes, on a logarithmic scale. The dark blob in inland South Australia is the Riverland, which alone crushed more than 443,000 tonnes in 2024 — roughly 35 times the entire Tasmanian harvest. Hover any region to see its name, state, total crush, and average wine rating.

Figure 1. Australian wine regions coloured by 2024 grape crush (tonnes, log scale). Source: Wine Australia.

The takeaway: Australian wine is a southern coastal story. The map's empty middle is not missing data — it is the desert.

What Grapes We Grow

If Australian wine had one signature, it would be Shiraz. Across the 2,329 Australian reviews in this dataset, Shiraz appears more than three times as often as the next most-reviewed variety — Chardonnay. Cabernet Sauvignon, Riesling and Pinot Noir round out the top five, with a long tail of blends and varietal experiments behind them.

But the headline figure hides regional character. The heatmap below the bar chart cross-tabulates variety against region: dark cells mark where a region has a strong concentration of reviews for a particular grape. Patterns leap out — Barossa Valley and McLaren Vale are unambiguously Shiraz country, Margaret River dominates Cabernet Sauvignon and Chardonnay, while Eden Valley and Clare Valley are the homes of Australian Riesling.

Figure 2. Top 15 grape varieties by number of expert reviews. Source: WineEnthusiast.

Figure 3. Variety × region matrix. Cell colour shows review concentration; filtered to regions and varieties with at least 30 reviews.

Why regional specialisation matters: a region's grape choice is rarely arbitrary — it reflects decades of trial-and-error matching soil and microclimate to varietal.

How Much We Make

The production landscape is dominated by one state: South Australia accounts for more wine than the rest of the country combined, crushing somewhere between 700,000 and 1,000,000 tonnes annually depending on the season. New South Wales and Victoria sit in the middle band. Western Australia, Queensland, and Tasmania trail well behind — Tasmania alone produces only about 12,000 tonnes, less than two per cent of South Australia's output.

The chart below uses a logarithmic y-axis precisely because of this disparity: on a linear scale, Tasmania's line would barely be visible. The log scale lets all six states share one chart while preserving the proportional differences. The 2025 figures shown are Wine Australia's forecast.

Figure 4. Total grape crush by state, 2020–2025. Log scale on y-axis. 2025 is forecast.

A note on scale: the 50× gap between South Australia and Tasmania is structural — climate, land cost, and bulk-wine infrastructure all favour the Riverland.

Does Price Predict Quality?

The conventional answer is yes, but the data tells a richer story. Each of the 2,329 dots on the chart below is a single Australian wine reviewed by WineEnthusiast critics. The price axis is logarithmic, the score axis runs from 80 to 100. There is a clear upward trend — expensive wines score higher on average — but the spread at every price point is enormous.

The most interesting story is in the lower price bracket: at $20–$40 USD, scores range across nearly the whole spectrum, from 82 to 95+. That means plenty of moderately priced Australian wines outperform bottles costing four or five times as much. Hover any dot to see the wine, winery, and region behind it.

Figure 5. Each dot is one of 2,329 Australian wines; x-axis on a log scale. Prices in USD.

The practical implication: price is a signal, not a guarantee. The cheapest path to a 92+ score wine runs through good regional sourcing, not big spending.

Climate Shapes Character

Plotting each region by its growing degree days (a standard measure of heat accumulated over the growing season) against its average reviewer score reveals one of the most consistent patterns in this entire dataset. Cooler regions cluster up and to the left; warmer regions sit lower and to the right. The size of each circle corresponds to 2024 crush — and the biggest circles, the volume producers, almost all sit in the warm, lower-scoring zone.

This is the central tension of Australian wine. The country's biggest producers (Riverland, Murray Darling, Riverina) are warm, irrigated, and built for scale. The country's highest-scoring regions (Tasmania, Yarra Valley, Adelaide Hills, Eden Valley) are cool, marginal, and built for character. The two rarely overlap.

Figure 6. Region-level bubble chart: warmth (x), avg score (y), 2024 crush (size), state (colour).

Cool climate makes the quality. Warm climate makes the volume. The trade-off defines the shape of the industry.

Which States Are Most Consistent?

An average score hides as much as it reveals. The box plots below show the full distribution of reviewer scores for each state — the middle 50% of wines sits inside the box, the line is the median, and the whiskers reach to the minimum and maximum. A short, high box means tight quality consistency; a long, low box means a wide variation between everyday wines and standout bottles.

The pattern matches what we saw in the climate chart. Tasmania and Victoria tend to have tighter, higher distributions — small industries built around premium production. South Australia has a wider spread, simply because it makes everything from $8 cleanskins to $400 Penfolds Grange.

Figure 7. Distribution of reviewer scores by state. Box = middle 50%; line = median; whiskers = min/max.

For the consumer: if you want a near-guaranteed good bottle, narrow the search to a cool-climate state.
